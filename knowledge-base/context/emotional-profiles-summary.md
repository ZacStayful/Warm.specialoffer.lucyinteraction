# Emotional Profile Archetypes — Summary Reference
*Built from 50 meetings | Schema 3.0 | Updated 2026-05-31*

---

## OVERVIEW

8 psychological archetypes are used to classify leads. Archetype determines the *communication style* and *objection response strategy* — not the content of the pitch, which remains consistent.

**Important**: Only 7/50 leads in the dataset have formally confirmed archetypes. The remaining are inferred. Treat all archetype assignments as working hypotheses, updated by new meeting behaviour.

---

## THE 8 ARCHETYPES

---

### 1. CERTAINTY-SEEKER
**Dataset count**: 1 confirmed  
**Core driver**: They need to know *exactly* what happens, when, and how, before they commit to anything. Ambiguity = anxiety = no decision.

**Identification signals**:
- Asks point-by-point questions about the agreement
- Wants specific examples ("What would happen if X?")
- Requests written confirmations before verbal commitment
- Revisits the same question in different ways
- "I just want to be sure that..." appears frequently

**What works**:
- Specificity. Exact figures, exact timelines, exact processes.
- Written follow-up summarising what was discussed
- "Here's exactly what happens after you sign: [step 1], [step 2], [step 3]"
- Patience — don't rush the close. They close when every question is answered.
- Reference: "We've done this with [X] properties in [location]. Here's what happened."

**What doesn't work**:
- Vague reassurance ("Don't worry, it'll all be fine")
- Rushing to close before questions are resolved
- Overpromising on income
- Skipping the agreement walkthrough

**Closing approach**:
"Let me take you through the agreement section by section so there are no surprises. Once you're satisfied everything is clear, we can confirm the start date."

### 2. LOSS-AVERSE SWITCHER
**Dataset count**: 2 confirmed  
**Core driver**: Primarily motivated by avoiding a worse outcome (losing income, losing control, making a mistake) rather than gaining a better one. Currently with a competitor and the pain of staying is slowly overtaking the risk of switching.

**Identification signals**:
- Currently with a management company
- Focuses heavily on what went wrong with current/previous manager
- Asks: "How do I know you won't do the same thing?"
- Compares every feature to their current situation
- "I just don't want to go through that again"

**What works**:
- Acknowledge the past experience before pitching
- Concrete difference evidence (individual listing, no markups, specific comms model)
- "The reason [bad thing] happens at [type of company] is [reason]. Here's how our model prevents that: [specific]."
- Offer to speak with an existing client who switched from similar situation
- Gradual trust-building — don't rush to offer

**What doesn't work**:
- Criticising the competitor by name
- Dismissing their bad experience ("That's unusual — most managers are fine")
- Jumping straight to income figures before addressing the trust issue
- Pressure to decide quickly

**Closing approach**:
"I understand why you'd be cautious — you've been let down. The best thing I can offer is transparency: here's exactly how we work, here's what you'll see every month, and here's a client you can speak to who was in a similar situation."

### 3. ANALYTICAL EVALUATOR
**Dataset count**: 3 confirmed (most common confirmed archetype)  
**Core driver**: Data and logic. They want to model the decision correctly before committing. Trust is earned through precision, not rapport.

**Identification signals**:
- Asks for exact breakdowns (gross → net calculation, platform fee structure)
- Challenges estimates: "How did you arrive at that number?"
- Takes notes during the call
- Asks about edge cases ("What if there's a month with no bookings?")
- Wants to compare STR vs LTL vs selling on a spreadsheet
- Questions the methodology, not just the conclusion

**What works**:
- Exact numbers, not ranges ("approximately £1,600")
- Show the working: "That's based on [X] comparable properties in [location] achieving [Y] gross, minus 15% management fee and ~12% platform costs, net £Z"
- Best/worst/average scenario modelling
- Acknowledge uncertainty honestly: "December and January are slower — this is the annual average"
- Let them probe. Don't get defensive.

**What doesn't work**:
- Round numbers without methodology
- "Trust me, it'll work"
- Rushing past their questions
- Overpromising on income (they will fact-check)

**Closing approach**:
"I'll send you the breakdown in writing so you can model it yourself. If the numbers stack up for you — and I think they will — let's confirm the start date on our next call."

### 4. URGENCY-DRIVEN
**Dataset count**: 0 confirmed (inferred in several READY_NOW leads)  
**Core driver**: Has a timeline pressure that makes inaction costly. Motivated by "I need to sort this before [event]". Responds well to deadline-anchored close.

**Identification signals**:
- Departure date / completion date / tenancy end within 6 weeks
- "I need to get this sorted before [X]"
- "I don't have a lot of time to make this decision"
- High engagement, quick responses, less questioning
- DLY-MOV leads with confirmed departure often exhibit this

**What works**:
- Anchor to their timeline: "So if we sign this week, you'll be live before you go"
- Create the path: "Here's what needs to happen in the next 2 weeks to hit that date"
- Remove friction: "I'll send the agreement today, you just need to sign it"
- Avoid overloading with information — they want clarity, not volume

**What doesn't work**:
- Lengthy discovery phase when they're ready to go
- Over-qualification when the readiness signals are already clear
- Slowing them down with caveats when they're trying to move fast

**Closing approach**:
"Let me make this straightforward: sign the agreement this week, I'll start the onboarding. You'll be live before [date]. Is that the right call?"

### 5. STATUS-QUO RESISTANT
**Dataset count**: 0 confirmed  
**Core driver**: Change is inherently risky. Their current situation, however imperfect, is known. Any new arrangement introduces unknowns they'd rather avoid.

**Identification signals**:
- "It's working well enough at the moment"
- "I don't want to rock the boat"
- "I'd need to be really sure before making any changes"
- Currently in a stable arrangement (long-let or self-managing OK)
- Passive engagement — polite but not enthusiastic

**What works**:
- "What would make NOT switching a bad decision?" (reframe the default as a cost, not safety)
- Concrete loss framing: "By staying long-let, you're leaving [X] per month on the table"
- Low-friction entry: emphasise how easy the transition is, how little disruption there is
- Time-limited social proof: "We onboarded 3 properties in [location] this quarter"

**What doesn't work**:
- Pressure or urgency framing (makes them defensive)
- Highlighting what they're missing (triggering avoidance, not action)
- Complex comparisons — keep it simple

**Closing approach**:
"I'm not asking you to change everything — I'm asking whether the difference in income justifies trying it for 6 months. At the end of 6 months, if it's not working, you give us 3 months notice. That's your downside."

### 6. BETRAYAL-DAMAGED
**Dataset count**: 0 confirmed (present in several EX-STL and GL-MGMT leads by description)  
**Core driver**: Had a genuinely bad experience with a previous STR manager. The wound is recent. Any new operator is viewed through the lens of "will they do what the last one did?"

**Identification signals**:
- Detailed description of specific failures (damage not fixed, reviews mismanaged, comms dropped)
- Emotional language about previous experience
- "My last manager..." followed by specifics
- Questions that probe for failure modes: "What happens if you don't fix something?"
- High skepticism, even when the surface conversation is friendly

**What works**:
- Stop. Listen fully. Don't interrupt. Let them get it out.
- Acknowledge: "That's a serious issue — [repeat back what they described]. I understand why that would put you off."
- Directly and specifically address the failure mode: "Here's how our model prevents that: [specific process, not platitude]"
- Offer evidence: existing client testimonial, Slack message screenshots, maintenance log example
- Slower close — they need more time than average

**What doesn't work**:
- Rushing past the bad experience to the pitch
- "That's unusual — most companies don't do that" (minimising their experience)
- Naming/criticising the competitor
- Same-day close — this archetype needs a second meeting

**Closing approach**:
"I'm not going to ask you to trust us on day one — that's earned. What I'd suggest is: review the agreement, speak to one of our clients who came from a similar situation, and let's talk again. The model stands up to scrutiny."

### 7. SOCIAL-PROOF DEPENDENT
**Dataset count**: 0 confirmed  
**Core driver**: Decisions are validated by what others like them have done. Testimonials, case studies, and referrals carry more weight than logic or numbers alone.

**Identification signals**:
- "Do you have any reviews I can look at?"
- "Do you have clients in [my area] I could speak to?"
- Mentions of friends who use STR management
- Asks about number of properties managed, years in business
- Receptive to case studies and specific success stories

**What works**:
- Lead with credibility signals: "6 years, ~70 properties, 40% direct booking"
- Specific testimonial matching: "We have a client in [similar location] with a [similar] property who's been with us 18 months — I can connect you"
- Area-specific social proof: "We manage [X] properties in [their location]"
- Response to doubt: "Would it help to speak to one of our clients directly?"

**What doesn't work**:
- Pure numbers without any social validation
- "Just trust us" without supporting evidence
- Failing to offer client introductions when asked

**Closing approach**:
"Let me send you a couple of client testimonials from similar properties, and I can arrange a quick intro call if you'd like. Once you've heard from them, I think the decision is easier."

---

### 8. FAST-PATH GAIN-FOCUSED
**Dataset count**: 1 confirmed  
**Core driver**: Primarily motivated by the upside and wants to move fast. Not particularly interested in detail — focused on the outcome. Over-explanation feels like friction.

**Identification signals**:
- "How quickly can we get started?"
- "What's the fee? OK, let's do it."
- High energy, fast decisions, low need for detail
- "I just want to know it's going to make money"
- Self-confident — not a questioner

**What works**:
- Clear, simple value statement first
- Shortest possible path to signing
- Remove friction from every step
- One clear ask: "Sign this, and we'll have you live within 3 weeks"
- Don't over-explain — they'll ask if they want more

**What doesn't work**:
- Long discovery phases
- Excessive caveats and worst-case scenarios
- Complex methodology explanations
- Slowing them down when they're ready to move

**Closing approach**:
"Good. Here's the agreement. Once you've signed, we'll get started. Any questions, call me direct."
